# Traces of the Deep Reef

Description:

Traces of the Deep Reef is a mobile application for collectors of early oceanographic research instruments, marine cartographers, and preservers of nautical science heritage, dedicated to cataloging the manual water-sampling bottles, mechanical depth-sounding leads, and analog marine sediment dredges used to map the ocean floor before the era of digital sonar and remote-controlled robotic submersibles.
Systematize your collection of the trackers of the currents—from brass Negretti and Zambra reversing thermometers that locked in deep-sea temperatures to heavy cast-lead sounding weights with tallow-cavities for collecting sea-floor mud, from early mechanical slip-water bottles to mechanical continuous-line current drift meters.
Create a digital archive of heavy industry by recording the instrument type, manufacturer, temperature range, era, and the specific steel mill, foundry, or ceramic kiln each specimen once calibrated.

## Bespoke Object Fields (Model "Oceanographic Tool"):

•	Oceanic Registry Log: A unique, system-generated marine-expedition tracking code (e.g., TDR-REEF-6602-DEEP-W).

•	Instrument Classification: The specific scientific category of the marine apparatus (e.g., Reversing Thermometer, Sounding Lead, Bottom Clam Dredge, Slip-Water Bottle, Drift Indicator).

•	Artisan Hallmark: Fictionalized nautical instrument smiths and scientific optical shops (e.g., Nautilus Scientific Instrument Co., DeepSea Marine Optics, IronAnchor Brassworks).

•	Sounding & Pressure Bounds: The max depth or hydro-pressure tolerances the instrument can survive (e.g., 500 Fathoms, 100 Atmospheres, 2000-Meter Suspension Limit).

•	Valving & Sealing Mechanics: For water samplers, the physical method used to isolate the deep-sea sample (e.g., Drop-weight messenger release, rotative plug-cock valve, spring-loaded rubber discs).

•	Ballast & Mass Profile: The weight system needed to pull the tool straight down through heavy currents (e.g., 14-lb split-lead weight, integrated bronze stabilizing fins).

•	Composition Metallurgy: The primary construction material protecting the tool from salt-water corrosion (e.g., Heavily tinned marine brass, solid gunmetal bronze, monel-metal linkages).

•	Physical Proportions: The absolute length of the thermometer sleeve or volume capacity of the dredge bucket, along with total dry weight.

•	Preservation Soundness: The physical state of the instrument (e.g., Salt-crust scoring, mercury column separation, spring tension scaling).

•	Expedition Ground Zero: The fictionalized oceanic survey route, arctic mapping voyage, or coastal research station where the tool logged data (e.g., Historic North Atlantic current mapping, forgotten south-seas coral survey, abandoned cliffside marine observatory warehouse).
